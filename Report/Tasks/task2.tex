\section{Task 2: Classification after Record Deletion (by Maria)}

\subsection{Overview}
In Task 1 we observed that most attributes show strong overlap of the
$Y = 0$ and $Y = 1$ distributions, which limits the SVM baseline
accuracy to $0.7576 \pm 0.0277$. Task 2 investigates the effect of
\emph{record deletion}: rows whose chosen attribute falls in a central
overlap corridor are removed from the dataset, artificially creating
a clearer class separation. The same 10-run GridSearch SVM pipeline as
in Task 1c is then re-applied to the cleaned data, and the resulting
accuracy is compared with the Task 1 baseline.

\subsection{Task 2a: Attribute Choice for Density Analysis}
Two attributes are chosen for the 2-dimensional density (scatter)
analysis: \textbf{alcohol} (X axis) and \textbf{pH} (Y axis).

The choice follows the spirit of Fig.~2 in the assignment, which pairs
a \emph{separating} attribute with an \emph{overlapping} one:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Alcohol} was identified in Task 1b as the strongest
    discriminator: bad wines concentrate around 9--10\%, good wines
    extend up to 12--13\%.
    \item \textbf{pH} was identified as one of the least informative
    attributes, with nearly complete overlap between classes.
\end{itemize}
This pairing makes it possible to delete a corridor along the alcohol
axis to enforce visible class separation, while pH provides
contextual spread in the orthogonal direction.

\subsection{Task 2b: Density Analysis and Record Deletion}
Figure~\ref{fig:scatter_before} shows the scatter plot before any
deletion. The two classes are intermixed in the central region
$\text{alcohol} \in [10.0, 10.7]$, while a slight class trend is
visible: $Y = 0$ tends to dominate below 10\% alcohol and $Y = 1$
above 10.7\%.

    Blue: $Y = 0$ (bad); orange: $Y = 1$ (good).}

\paragraph{Deletion rule.}
All records whose alcohol value falls in the central corridor
$[10.0, 10.7]$ are removed, irrespective of class. This is the
\emph{symmetric corridor} approach described in the lecture
(slides 7--11): records are deleted in a value range where the two
class histograms overlap, so that the remaining histograms become
separated.

\paragraph{Effect of the deletion.}
The original dataset contains 1143 rows (522 bad / 621 good). After
removing the corridor, 889 rows remain (415 bad / 474 good): 254 rows
(22.2\%) are deleted, and the class balance is preserved. Figure
\ref{fig:scatter_before_after} compares the scatter plots before and
after the deletion. After cleaning, a visible vertical gap appears in
the alcohol axis, and the two class clouds are noticeably more
separated.

\subsection{Task 2c: Ten Runs of Modelling and Testing on the Cleaned Data}

\paragraph{Pipeline.}
The pipeline, hyperparameter grid and number of runs are deliberately
\emph{identical} to Task 1c: \texttt{StandardScaler} followed by
\texttt{SVC}, the same set of 50 hyperparameter combinations searched
by \texttt{GridSearchCV} with 5-fold cross-validation, 10 outer runs
with stratified 80/20 splits. The only difference between Task 1c and
Task 2c is the input data, so any change in accuracy can be attributed
to the record deletion alone.

The mean accuracy increases by $+1.4$ percentage points (about
$+1.8\%$ in relative terms). At the same time, the standard deviation
drops from 0.0277 to 0.0183, a reduction of roughly one third: the
model has become noticeably more \emph{stable} across runs, even
though the mean accuracy gain is moderate.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
The effect of record deletion is real but limited. The SVM in Task 1c
already had access to all 11 attributes and used the redundant
information among them. Removing the central alcohol corridor cleans
the decision boundary along that one dimension, but the other 10
attributes provide overlapping discriminative information, which
partially absorbs the effect. The drop in standard deviation indicates
that the deleted rows were also the most \emph{ambiguous} rows for the
classifier: removing them reduces the dependence of the result on the
random train/test split.

\subsection{Conclusion}
Symmetric corridor deletion along alcohol increases the SVM accuracy
from $0.7576 \pm 0.0277$ to $0.7713 \pm 0.0183$. The gain in mean
accuracy is modest ($+1.4$ p.p.), but the run-to-run stability of the
model improves clearly. This confirms that record deletion is a
working but moderate feature engineering technique on this dataset,
since most discriminative information is already captured by the
remaining attributes.
